A Beginner's Guide To Watch Features

February 21, 2020 10:58 AM

share
  • Listen to this article

**Time**

Using a watch to tell the time may sound so incredibly basic that it’s not worth mentioning, but given the amount of ways in which time can be displayed (and, in some very perplexing cases, sometimes not at all) it is worth pointing out the obvious.

Watches that tell the time using hands are known as analog watches. They are very common in the luxury watch mark. The most standard handset (family of hands) you will find on a watch will comprise of an hour, a minute, and a seconds hand. The hour is generally short and stout, the minute long and slightly narrower, and the seconds hand tends to be shaped like a needle, often with a more prominent tip like an arrow or dot of luminous material sometimes referred to as a lollipop hand.

Time can also be told digitally with just the use of numbers (imagine your common alarm clock format). Interestingly these watches can also be mechanically driven, although the vast, vast majority are battery powered, with their time regulated by a quartz crystal.

**Date**

Functions beyond the time are referred to as complications in the field of watchmaking. Somewhat confusingly, not all complications are that complicated. The date, which some people take for granted, is normally the first complication to be added to a watch. Do remember to check if your watch _does_ have a date, function however, as it isn’t guaranteed. For some types of watches, the date makes a lot of sense (like GMT watches, for example, as those the GMT complication compliments the date function very nicely), but on other styles of watches (such as dive watches) the date is not necessary, and may even be seen as an unwanted distraction. The date, when presented through a window (dial cut-out) is normally located at 3, between 4 and 5, or at 6 o’clock, but it can be anywhere the watchmaker wants assuming they can adjust the movement layout accordingly. The date can also be indicated by way of a hand, either pointing to a number printed around the outside of the dial, or contained within a “sub-dial” which is a separate miniature dial within the dial where additional information can be displayed.

**Day/Date**

Building on the simple date complication, the day is sometimes added. It is common for date discs to be printed in two languages (most commonly English and French, but Japanese is the common alternative for cheaper Japanese made movements). It is also normal to see the abbreviation of Sunday (SUN) colored in red (or sometimes blue). Because of the added space and reduced flexibility of the day complication it is normally displayed at either 3 (very common) or 6 o’clock. Less commonly, as with the Junghans Meister Calendar, it is displayed in a window of its own, and in very complicated watches it occasionally features alongside the month in a 12 o’clock sub-dial. It is also possible to display the day of the week in a sub-dial, but this is also quite seldom seen.

**GMT**

A GMT function enables the wearer to simultaneously display two time zones on their watch at once. Several very high profile GMT watches that utilizes a rotating external bezel as well as a GMT hand are actually able to show three time zones at once, but reading the time that way is somewhat less intuitive. A simple GMT function uses one extra hand that is geared to run at half the speed of the hour hand, so it is running on a 24-hour cycle (one rotation of the dial per day as opposed to two full rotations that the hour hand would normally make). A secondary benefit to a GMT hand is that if it is synchronized with your normal home time, it works as an am/pm indicator (if you happen to find yourself trapped in a box and don’t know if its daylight or not).

**Power Reserve**

The power reserve is a very simple complication mostly found on manually wound watches that need to be rewound every day to prevent them from running out of power. The power reserve is a fuel gauge, and will remind you to wind your watch when the power gets critically low should you forget. Power reserves are featured on automatic watches, but are less essential due to the watch remaining fully wound thanks to the movement of the wearer’s wrist.

**Moon Phase**

A moon phase indicator shows the phase of the moon as it waxes and wanes from a new moon, to a full moon, and back again. The cycle takes roughly 29.5 days so requires creative gearing to display accurately. Normally a moon phase disc has 59 teeth and two moons printed on it (so one full rotation of the disc is good for two complete phases of the moon).

The moon phase complication is one of the most beautiful on the market. They come in all shapes and sizes, but are normally contained within a sub-dial at 6 o’clock, although other locations do exist. There is a big difference between entry-level moon phase complications and the very advanced technology going into some seriously high-end pieces today. For the most part, a simple moon phase indicator is more than accurate enough for the casual lunatic, but some of the very best are capable of remaining precise for more than a hundred years.
